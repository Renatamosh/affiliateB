When someone says "I play bridge," they almost certainly mean **contract bridge** - the most widely played and strategically sophisticated form of the card game. Understanding what makes it "contract" bridge helps clarify why bidding accurately is the heart of the game.

## What Makes It "Contract" Bridge?

The defining feature of contract bridge is this: **you only score game-level trick points for tricks you specifically bid**.

If you bid 2♠ and make 10 tricks, you score:
- **Trick points**: only 2 × 30 = 60 (the 2 tricks you bid)
- **Overtricks**: 2 × 30 = 60 above the line (bonuses, not toward game)

Contrast this with **auction bridge** (the predecessor): in auction bridge, all tricks won counted toward game, regardless of how many were bid. This made aggressive bidding unnecessary - you could bid low and still score everything you made.

Contract bridge changed this entirely. Now, only bid tricks count toward game. This one rule transformed bridge from a scoring exercise into a bidding game of precision and judgment.

## A Brief History

### From Whist to Bridge

Bridge evolved from Whist, the dominant card game of 19th-century England. The key innovations:

- **Bridge Whist** (~1890s): introduced the concept of one player (Dummy) laying their hand face-up
- **Auction Bridge** (1904): introduced competitive bidding - players bid for the right to name trumps
- **Contract Bridge** (1925): Harold Vanderbilt's refinement, making bid contracts the only path to game

### Harold Vanderbilt's Invention (1925)

In November 1925, Harold Stirling Vanderbilt - wealthy American sportsman and later America's Cup defender - was aboard the steamship SS Finland travelling from Los Angeles through the Panama Canal to Havana.

During the voyage, Vanderbilt and fellow passengers developed and refined a new scoring system based on the French game **Plafond** (which already required bidding the contract). Vanderbilt added:
- The concept of **vulnerability** (bonus/penalty stakes)
- Refined **slam bonuses**
- The **game/rubber structure** that remains today

He distributed written rules to his friends after the cruise. Within five years, contract bridge had replaced auction bridge at most clubs. Within ten, it dominated card play worldwide.

## Bridge Variants Compared

All variants use identical bidding and card play rules. They differ in format and scoring.

| Variant | Format | Scoring | Where Played |
|---------|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Rubber Bridge** | Continuous until one side wins two games | Aggregate points, rubber bonus | Home games, some clubs |
| **Chicago / 4-Deal** | Fixed 4-deal session | Aggregate points, no rubber | Home games, some clubs |
| **Duplicate Pairs** | Pre-dealt boards, comparison scoring | Matchpoints | Bridge clubs |
| **Duplicate Teams** | Pre-dealt boards, margin scoring | IMPs | Tournaments |
| **Online Bridge** | Flexible | Varies by platform | BBO, Funbridge, RealBridge |

### Rubber Bridge

The traditional form. A rubber consists of multiple deals until one partnership wins two games. Scoring accumulates above and below a horizontal line. The rubber ends with a bonus of 500 (if opponent won one game) or 700 (if opponent won no games).

### Chicago / 4-Deal Bridge

Designed to make home sessions a fixed length. Each session is exactly 4 deals, with pre-assigned vulnerability:
- **Deal 1**: Neither side vulnerable
- **Deal 2**: Dealer's side vulnerable
- **Deal 3**: Non-dealer's side vulnerable
- **Deal 4**: Both sides vulnerable

After 4 deals, the game ends and scores are totalled. Popular because sessions last a predictable amount of time and everyone gets to deal.

### Duplicate Bridge

The competitive form used at clubs and tournaments. The same pre-dealt hands are played by multiple pairs, and scores are compared. See our full guide to [duplicate bridge](/learn-bridge/duplicate-bridge-explained/).

## The Four Suits

Bridge uses a standard 52-card deck, with cards ranked Ace (highest) down to 2 (lowest) within each suit.

| Suits | Category | Bid rank | Trick score |
|-------|----------|----------|-------------|
| ♠ Spades | Major | 4th (highest) | 30 per trick |
| ♥ Hearts | Major | 3rd | 30 per trick |
| ♦ Diamonds | Minor | 2nd | 20 per trick |
| ♣ Clubs | Minor | 1st (lowest) | 20 per trick |

**No-Trump** ranks above all suits in bidding and scores 40 for the first trick, 30 for each subsequent trick.

The major/minor distinction matters in bidding strategy: major-suit games (4♥ or 4♠) require only 10 tricks, while minor-suit games (5♣ or 5♦) require 11 tricks.
