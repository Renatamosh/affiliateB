Bridge etiquette is not about being formal. It is about protecting the integrity of the game and ensuring that everyone - beginners and experts alike - enjoys the experience. Most breaches of etiquette are unintentional, but knowing the standards helps you avoid them.

## The Core Principle: Fair Information Only

The single most important etiquette rule in bridge is this: **convey information to your partner only through your legal calls and plays, nothing else.**

Your partner is entitled to know the agreed meaning of your bids and leads - nothing more. Any extra signal you send, even unintentionally, is called **unauthorized information**, and acting on it violates the Laws of Bridge.

Common sources of unauthorized information to avoid:

- Hesitating noticeably before a routine bid
- Sighing, grimacing, or raising an eyebrow when your partner bids
- Saying "interesting" or "really?" after an opponent's bid
- Playing a card with unusual speed or slowness on a critical trick
- Reaching toward a card and then withdrawing

If you accidentally receive unauthorized information from your partner (e.g., they hesitated before passing), you are obligated to make the action that your hand alone would suggest - even if it costs you a good result.

## Bidding Conduct

**Bid in turn.** The auction proceeds clockwise. Never bid, pass, or double before it is your turn.

**Use a consistent tempo.** Varying your speed - thinking for 30 seconds on a routine pass - communicates information to your partner about hand strength or distribution even when your call says nothing.

**Use correct terminology.** Say "one no-trump" or "double" clearly. Mumbled or ambiguous calls cause confusion and may be subject to Director rulings.

**Alert your partner's conventional bids.** In duplicate bridge, many conventions require you to alert the opponents that the bid is not natural. Failure to alert, or alerting when you should not, can lead to adjusted scores.

**Do not consult the convention card during the auction.** You may look at your own convention card before the auction begins or during the play. Looking during the auction signals uncertainty and may give your partner information.

## Play Conduct

**Play in turn.** Do not play a card until it is your turn, even if the correct play is obvious.

**Do not comment on the play.** No "good lead," "bad luck," or "I knew it" while the hand is in progress. Let the cards speak.

**Keep your cards private.** Do not let opponents see your hand by holding it too high or fanning it carelessly.

**Do not detach a card prematurely.** In bridge, once a card is led, it is played. Handling cards as if you are about to play before your turn is a minor breach that can escalate.

**Claim correctly.** If you can see you will win all remaining tricks (or a stated number), you may claim by laying your hand face-up and announcing your line of play. State your intended play clearly - an unclear or incorrect claim may be adjudicated by the Director.

## Dummy's Conduct

Dummy has a specific, limited role once the opening lead is made:

| Dummy may | Dummy may not |
|-----------|---------------|
| Watch the play attentively | Suggest plays to Declarer |
| Draw attention to a possible revoke by Declarer | Comment on opponents' plays |
| Ask if a player has no card of the suit led | Look at a Defender's hand without permission |
| Keep track of tricks won and lost | Participate in the play in any way |

Violating Dummy's conduct rules - especially commenting on the play - can result in a Director ruling against the Declaring side.

## Handling Errors

Mistakes happen at every level of bridge. How you handle them defines your reputation at the table.

**In duplicate bridge:** Call the Director immediately for any irregularity - a bid out of turn, a revoke, a lead out of turn, a claim dispute. Do not try to fix it yourself. The Director applies the Laws of Bridge and ensures both sides are treated fairly.

**In a casual game:** Agree on a simple, fair remedy between the four players and move on. The spirit of the game matters more than a strict ruling.

**After an error by your partner:** Do not criticise, sigh heavily, or replay the hand at the table. A good partner says nothing until the session is over, then discusses it constructively in private.

## General Club Etiquette

- Arrive on time. Late arrivals delay the entire field.
- Switch off or silence your phone.
- Thank your opponents with a handshake or "well played" at the end of each round.
- Keep post-hand discussion brief at the table - others are waiting.
- Do not kibbitz (watch others play) unless invited, and never comment on what you see.
- Dress appropriately for the club's standard.

Etiquette in bridge is less a set of rules and more a shared commitment to a game worth playing well. Follow these standards, treat your partners and opponents with respect, and you will always be a welcome addition to any table.
