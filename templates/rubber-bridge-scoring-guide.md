# How to Keep Score in Rubber Bridge: The Complete Scoring Guide

Rubber bridge scoring intimidates a lot of players — not because it's complicated, but because nobody ever sat down and explained it clearly in one place. The score pad looks simple until you're staring at it after a 4 Spades bid and you're not sure which number goes where and why.

This guide fixes that. Whether you're hosting your first bridge evening or you've been playing for years and quietly nodding along while someone else kept score, here is everything you need to know — the score pad, trick points, vulnerability, game and rubber bonuses, slams, penalties, and a full reference table you can print and keep on the table.

If you're still getting your home game set up, see our full guide on [how to host a bridge evening at home](/how-to/how-to-host-a-bridge-evening-at-home) first.

## The Score Pad: What That Line Actually Means

The bridge score pad has two columns — "We" and "They" — and a horizontal line drawn across the middle of both. This line is the most important feature of the whole pad. Everything in rubber bridge scoring flows from understanding which side of the line a score belongs on.

**Below the line** is where you record trick points — the points earned for making the contract you bid. Only trick points count toward winning a game. The target is 100 points below the line, and the first side to reach it wins a game.

**Above the line** is where everything else goes: overtricks (extra tricks beyond your contract), bonuses for slams, bonuses for honors, penalties when the opposing side fails to make their contract, and eventually the rubber bonus when the rubber is won. These points matter for the final total but do not count toward winning a game.

A good set of [bridge score pads](https://www.amazon.com/Contract-Bridge-3-Pack-Printed-Scoring/dp/B07BWYRTB2) already has this line printed in. If you're improvising with a notebook, draw it before you start — doing it mid-rubber is asking for confusion.

## Trick Points: What Goes Below the Line

When a side makes their contract (takes the required number of tricks), the trick score for the exact number of tricks bid goes below the line. If they take extra tricks, those are overtricks and go above the line. Here are the trick point values:

| Suit | Points Per Trick (bid and made) |
|------|-------------------------------|
| Clubs (♣) | 20 per trick |
| Diamonds (♦) | 20 per trick |
| Hearts (♥) | 30 per trick |
| Spades (♠) | 30 per trick |
| No Trump (NT) | 40 for the 1st trick, 30 for each after |

Remember that only the tricks above the 6-trick "book" count. So a contract of 1 Spade requires 7 tricks (6 + 1) and scores 30 points. A contract of 2 Hearts requires 8 tricks and scores 60 points.

To make a game in one hand you need to bid and make 3 No Trump (40 + 30 + 30 = 100), 4 Hearts or 4 Spades (30 x 4 = 120), or 5 Clubs or 5 Diamonds (20 x 5 = 100). These are the game-level contracts. Anything below them scores a part-score — it counts toward game but doesn't get you there in one bid.

A well-known club story: a player once kept asking why their 2 No Trump made exactly scored 70 and not 100. After three careful explanations, their partner leaned over and said "Because you didn't bid enough." The player nodded sagely and asked again the following week.

---

## Part-Scores and How They Carry Forward

A part-score is any trick score below 100. If you bid and make 2 Spades, you score 60 below the line. Your 60 sits there, carrying forward to future hands. The next time your side makes a contract, those trick points add to the 60. Reach 100 combined and you've made a game.

Part-scores are accumulated until one side reaches 100. The moment one side makes game, a new horizontal line is drawn below the trick score, and both sides start fresh below the new line toward the next game. The part-score on the other side does not carry forward — it's wiped.

This is the part that trips people up most often. Your opponents had 60 below the line. You make a game. A new line goes under your game score. Their 60 is gone — it sits between the old line and the new one, inaccessible. They have to start again.

---

## Vulnerability: What It Means and Why It Matters

Once a side has won one game, they become **vulnerable** for the rest of the rubber. The other side, having won no games, is **not vulnerable**.

Vulnerability changes the stakes in two ways. It increases penalty points when you fail to make your contract (bad news if you bid too boldly), and it increases the bonuses you earn for slams (good news if you bid them and make them). It does not change trick points below the line.

This is why bridge players sometimes say vulnerability is the most important piece of information at the table. A side that is vulnerable has more to lose when they go down and more to gain when they succeed in the big contracts.

---

## What Goes Above the Line

Above the line is where the scoring gets interesting. Here is every category with the exact values.

### Overtrick Bonuses

When you make more tricks than your contract required, each extra trick (overtrick) scores above the line. The score per overtrick equals the trick point value of the suit — 20 for minors, 30 for majors, 30 for no trump. Vulnerability does not change overtrick scoring in rubber bridge (this is different from duplicate). So a major-suit overtrick scores 30 above the line whether you're vulnerable or not.

### Game Bonus

In rubber bridge, there is no standalone "game bonus" written above the line when you win a game — the reward for winning games is the rubber bonus, collected at the end. (Chicago bridge handles this differently, awarding an immediate game bonus per game won, which is one reason its scoring is slightly more complex.)

### Slam Bonuses

Slam bonuses are significant and very much worth bidding for.

| Slam Type | Not Vulnerable | Vulnerable |
|-----------|----------------|------------|
| Small Slam (12 tricks bid and made) | +500 | +750 |
| Grand Slam (all 13 tricks bid and made) | +1,000 | +1,500 |

These bonuses go above the line and can swing a rubber dramatically. A grand slam bid and made while vulnerable adds 1,500 points above the line before the trick score even counts. This is why your partner starts breathing heavily when they pick up a 20-count and an eight-card suit.

### Honors Bonus

If one player holds 4 of the 5 trump honors (A K Q J 10) in their own hand, their side scores 100 points above the line. If one player holds all 5 trump honors, it's 150 points. In a No Trump contract, holding all four aces in one hand scores 150 above the line. Honors are optional in some home games — agree before you start whether you're counting them.

### Penalty Scores (Undertricks)

When a side fails to make their contract, the declaring side goes "down" and the defenders score penalty points above the line. Here are the penalty values:

**Not Doubled:**

| Tricks Down | Not Vulnerable | Vulnerable |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| 1 | 50 | 100 |
| 2 | 100 | 200 |
| 3 | 150 | 300 |
| Each additional | +50 | +100 |

**Doubled:**

| Tricks Down | Not Vulnerable | Vulnerable |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| 1 | 100 | 200 |
| 2 | 300 | 500 |
| 3 | 500 | 800 |
| Each additional | +200 | +300 |

Redoubled penalties are double the doubled values. This is the territory where poor bidding gets expensive and rubber bridge develops its reputation as a game with genuine stakes.

---

## The Rubber Bonus: How the Rubber Ends

The rubber ends when one side wins their second game. That side earns a **rubber bonus**, scored above the line.

| Rubber Result | Bonus |
|---------------|-------|
| Win rubber 2-0 (opponents have no game) | +700 |
| Win rubber 2-1 (opponents have one game) | +500 |
| Unfinished rubber, one game to none | +300 |
| Unfinished rubber, part-score only | +100 |

An unfinished rubber is one where the players need to stop before a winner has emerged — whether the evening ran long, someone has to leave, or the host realized they forgot to put the kettle on. In that case, award the partial bonuses above and total everything up.

---

## How to Total the Rubber Score

When the rubber ends, add up all the points — both above and below the line — for each side. The side with the higher total wins the rubber. For money games, you divide the final margin by 100 and round to the nearest whole number to get the stake amount.

As an example: your side scores 120 below the line across two games, plus a 700 rubber bonus, 150 for a small slam, and 30 for one overtrick. That's 1,000 points total. Your opponents scored 60 below the line and 150 in penalties they collected on one of your contracts. Their total is 210. Your margin is 790 — rounded to 8 for a money game at 1 point per 100.

## A Note on Doubled and Redoubled Contracts Made

When a doubled contract is made, the trick points below the line are doubled (so a doubled 4 Spades made scores 240 below the line — an instant game with change to spare). The declaring side also collects a 50-point "insult bonus" above the line, because the opponents doubled them into a made game. Redoubled multiplies trick points by four, with a 100-point insult bonus.

This is why doubling a confident opponent into game can be one of the more expensive habits in bridge. The insult bonus is small compensation for watching them score 240 below the line in a single hand.

## Practice Tip: Run a Sample Rubber

The fastest way to get comfortable with scoring is to run a dummy rubber before your next home evening. Deal three or four hands, award the scores, track the part-scores, and draw the new line when you hit 100. Get the physical habit of reaching for the pencil and writing in the right column before your guests arrive. Once you've done it a few times without the pressure of a live game, the score pad stops being intimidating.

## FAQ

### How do you score rubber bridge for beginners?

Draw a horizontal line across the middle of the score pad. Trick points for making a contract go below the line. Overtricks, bonuses, slam bonuses, and penalties all go above the line. The first side to score 100 points below the line wins a game. Win two games and you win the rubber.

### What is below the line in rubber bridge?

Below the line is where you record the trick points for the contract you bid and made. Clubs and diamonds score 20 points per trick, hearts and spades score 30 per trick, and no trump scores 40 for the first trick then 30 for each additional. Only these points count toward making a game of 100.

### What does vulnerable mean in rubber bridge?

Vulnerable means your side has already won one game in the current rubber. Being vulnerable increases the penalty for going down in a contract and also increases slam bonuses. It raises the stakes in both directions — the risk and the reward both go up.

### How many points do you need to win rubber bridge?

You need to win two games. Each game requires 100 or more points accumulated below the line. The rubber bonus is 700 points if your opponents have no game, or 500 points if they won one game before you completed the rubber.

### What happens to a part-score at the end of a rubber bridge game?

If you have a part-score when your opponents complete a game, your part-score is wiped — a new line is drawn and you start fresh toward the next game. If the rubber ends before either side wins a second game, the side with a part-score earns a 100-point bonus above the line.
